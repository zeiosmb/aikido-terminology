## Core Concepts

### Mind / Spirit — Shin / Kokoro 心

Heart, mind, spirit.
- Mushin 無心 (empty / no-mind)
- Shoshin 初心 (beginner's mind)
- Zanshin 残心 (remaining / lingering mind, awareness after technique)
- Fudoshin 不動心 (immovable mind)
- Heijoshin 平常心 (everyday mind, equanimity)

### Ki 気

Energy, spirit, breath, intention. Central to the name *aikido* itself.
- Aiki 合気 (joining ki; harmonizing energy)
- Kiai 気合 (focused shout, projection of ki)
- Kihaku 気迫 (vigor, fighting spirit)
- Ki musubi 気結び (tying / connecting ki between partners)

### Hara / Tanden

- Hara 腹 (belly, center of gravity, locus of energy)
- Tanden 丹田 (energy center, ~2 inches below the navel)
- Seika tanden 臍下丹田 (the specific point below the navel)

### Victory — Katsu 勝

Win, gain victory.
- Masakatsu 正勝 (true / correct victory)
- Agatsu 吾勝 (self-victory)
- Katsuhayahi / Katsuhayabi 勝速日 (victory swift as the sun)
- Full saying: **Masakatsu Agatsu Katsuhayabi** 正勝吾勝勝速日 — Ueshiba's motto, "True victory is self-victory, victory in the speed of light."

### Correct / Just — Masa, Sei 正

- Masakatsu 正勝
- Seiza 正座 (correct sitting)
- Shomen 正面 — two senses:
  1. (a) front face (anatomical, as in *shomenuchi*)
  2. (b) the front of the dojo where the picture of O-Sensei resides (the direction faced for *shomen-ni-rei*)

### Fundamentals — Kihon 基本

- Kihon waza 基本技 (basic techniques)
- Kihon dōsa 基本動作 (basic movements)

### Other concepts

- Ma-ai 間合い (combative distance, interval)
- Awase 合わせ (blending, matching)
- Musubi 結び (connection, tying together)
- Mitori keiko 見取り稽古 (learning by watching)
- Kamae → Waza → Zanshin (stance → technique → finish)
- Shikaku 死角 (blind spot, dead angle; shi 死 = death, kaku 角 = angle)
- Misogi 禊 (purification ritual; Ueshiba practiced misogi heavily, as did Akira Tohei)
- Kotodama 言霊 (word-spirit; the belief that words and sounds carry spiritual force — to speak a sound is to invite its meaning. Foundational to Ueshiba's mystical teachings; also the cultural premise behind shi/ku number avoidance — see [Numerical superstition](#numerical-superstition--shi--ku))
Japanese cultural concepts (not aikido-specific, but they recur in dojo life):
- Giri 義理 (duty, obligation — a binding social debt that must be repaid)
- Honne 本音 vs. Tatemae 建前 (private true feelings vs. public face / outward conduct)

### Numerical superstition — shi & ku

Two numbers carry homophones with unwelcome meanings and are routinely avoided in speech:

- **四 (shi)** = 4 — homophone of **死 (shi)** = death
- **九 (ku)** = 9 — homophone of **苦 (ku)** = suffering, pain

The neutral readings **yon** (4) and **kyū** (9) are preferred in most contexts; *shi* and *ku* are softened or skipped. Practical consequences in Japanese culture:
- Hospital rooms and floors often skip 4 (and sometimes 9), similar to Western avoidance of 13
- Gifts are never given in quantities of 4
- Formal pricing, seating, and numbering routinely skip 4
- Sushi on a plate is arranged in two offset pairs rather than four in a line

In an aikido context:
- When *nage* throws *uke* four times in a basic drill, the count is structured as **two omote + two ura** rather than a flat "1-2-3-4" in sequence.
- Modern dojos count through *yon* without issue; the avoidance is of the sound *shi*, not the number itself.

This connects to **言霊 (kotodama)** — the belief that words and sounds carry spiritual force, so to *speak* a sound is to invite its meaning. Avoiding *shi* is not idle superstition but flows from a cultural premise that aligns naturally with Ueshiba's own kotodama-based teachings. (See [Core Concepts](#other-concepts).)

## Forms of Practice (Keiko 稽古)

- Keiko 稽古 (practice)
- Keikogi 稽古着 (practice uniform)
- Kihon waza 基本技 (basic techniques)
- Kakari keiko 掛かり稽古 (drill practice; same technique against successive uke)
- Gokaku keiko 互角稽古 ("evenly-matched practice"; partners of comparable technical level and physical capacity training together. 互角 = "even / on par," 稽古 = practice. Formally, this is the form of *ippan keiko* (ordinary practice) defined by parity between partners; experientially, equally-matched partners can let the ki-blending deepen — neither has to throttle back or reach upward, so the *musubi* (connection) finds its own level. A fulfilling, almost spiritual quality emerges from that meeting that doesn't arise the same way between mismatched partners.)
- Hikitate keiko 引立稽古 ("supportive practice"; the complement to gokaku keiko — a senior trains with a junior, helping the junior progress by allowing correct technique to land while neutralizing poor technique without forcing a fall. The senior "draws up" 引立 the junior.)
- Jiyu waza 自由技 (free practice; nage varies techniques against interval attacks)
- Henka waza 変化技 (changing technique; switch from one to another mid-flow)
- Kaeshi waza 返し技 (counter techniques; uke reverses nage's technique)
- Jiyu waza 自由技 (free practice; the freedom to choose and apply technique appropriately to each situation. **Preferred term in the Tohei/USAF lineage** — see [Jiyu Waza vs. Randori](#jiyu-waza-vs-randori) below.)
  - Ni nin gake 二人掛け (two-person attack; also read **futari-gake**)
  - San nin gake 三人掛け (three-person attack)
- Randori 乱取り (literally "chaos-taking"; freestyle multi-attacker practice. **Akira Tohei Sensei found this term unsatisfying** — he likened it to a food buffet: "a little here, a little there." He preferred *jiyu waza*. See below.)
- Mitori keiko 見取り稽古 (learning by watching)
- Kokoro keiko 心稽古 — also **Kokoro no keiko 心の稽古** ("practice of the heart/mind"; mental rehearsal and internalization of technique)
- Suwari waza 座り技 (seated technique)
- Hanmi handachi 半身半立ち (nage kneeling, uke standing)
- Tachi waza 立ち技 (standing technique)
- Ushiro waza 後技 (rear-attack techniques)

### Jiyu Waza vs. Randori

Two terms describe free-form practice, and they are not neutral synonyms.

**Randori 乱取り** — "chaos-taking." The kanji 乱 carries the sense of disorder, scattering, randomness. **Akira Tohei Sensei** (direct student of O-Sensei) found this term unsatisfying. He likened *randori* to a food buffet: *a little here, a little there* — picking and choosing techniques without intention or coherence. The word implies that whatever surfaces from chaos is what you take.

**Jiyu waza 自由技** — "free techniques." 自由 means *freedom*, *liberty*, *as it pleases you*. The emphasis is on the practitioner's **creative agency** — applying the right technique to the moment, not simply reacting to whatever flies at you. Tohei Sensei preferred this framing: free practice as the disciplined exercise of judgment, not as buffet sampling.

A technical USAF distinction also exists between the two:
- **Jiyu waza** — interval attacks; one uke at a time, nage varies techniques in response
- **Randori** — simultaneous attacks by multiple uke (the multi-attacker form on Kyu/Dan exams)

**An institutional note.** Akira Tohei Sensei was senior to Yoshimitsu Yamada Sensei and led the technical direction of the USAF until his death.

## Writing Systems & Romanization

### Japanese scripts

- **Kanji 漢字** — logographic characters borrowed from Chinese; each carries meaning. ~2,000 in common use. Most have multiple readings: *on-yomi* (Chinese-derived) and *kun-yomi* (native Japanese). Example: 心 reads *kokoro* (kun) or *shin* (on), both meaning "heart/mind."
- **Hiragana ひらがな** — phonetic syllabary, 46 base symbols + diacritics. Cursive, rounded shapes. Used for native words, grammar particles, and verb endings. Example: あ a, か ka, さ sa, た ta.
- **Katakana カタカナ** — phonetic syllabary, same 46 sounds as hiragana but angular shapes. Used for foreign loanwords, onomatopoeia, scientific names, and emphasis. Example: ア a, カ ka, サ sa, タ ta.
- **Rōmaji ローマ字** — Latin-alphabet representation of Japanese sounds.

A typical Japanese sentence mixes all three native scripts: 私は合気道を練習する (*Watashi wa aikidō o renshū suru* — "I practice aikido") — kanji carry the content words, hiragana the grammar.

### Romanization systems

This file uses **Hepburn** (ヘボン式 *Hebon-shiki*), the standard in English-language martial arts texts. Two other systems appear in older or more academic sources:

- **Hepburn** — phonetic-feeling for English readers (sh, ch, ts, f, j). Long vowels marked with macrons (ō, ū), often omitted in casual writing.
- **Kunrei-shiki 訓令式** — Japanese government standard, strictly systematic by syllable column.
- **Nihon-shiki 日本式** — older, even stricter than Kunrei (preserves historical distinctions like ぢ/じ).

**Long vowels** in Hepburn use macrons (ō, ū). In casual writing the macron is usually dropped (*aikido*, *kyu*, *shihonage*) — which is what most of this file does. Kunrei uses a circumflex (ô, û) instead.
